### **Case Study 0: The Original Build: Oil, Taxes, and the Making of Modern America**

**I. The Genesis of a System: Fueling an Industrial Revolution**

Imagine America at the turn of a new century. It was a nation of incredible energy, poised for explosive growth, but still finding its footing. For decades, the federal government largely ignored personal or corporate income when collecting money. Federal taxes were almost negligible, a barely visible line item in the national ledger, mostly relying on tariffs on imports. This meant the **Commonwealth's** ability to invest in big projects was severely limited. Crucially, **the entire national economy operated under the stringent constraints of the Gold Standard.** This system tethered the government's ability to spend directly to its physical gold reserves, much like a household's budget is tied to its savings. This created a constant, tight financial leash, limiting what the nation could build.

As America rapidly industrialized and its global influence grew, it became clear this old funding model wouldn't suffice. The nation needed a more stable and expansive way to fund its ambitions and meet the demands of a modern industrial power. This led to a monumental operational choice: **the modern federal income tax system effectively began with the Revenue Act of 1913\.** This single act didn't just add a new line to the books; it fundamentally re-engineered how the **Commonwealth** would fund itself. It shifted the source of public capital from tariffs to a direct, more robust claim on national income, creating a consistent revenue stream to build and maintain the shared assets that benefit every builder.

It was in this new era of financing a modern industrial power that the U.S. government, especially after oil proved utterly essential for winning World War I—fueling every ship, tank, and plane in a direct contribution to Allied victory—realized this wasn't just about private fortunes. **Oil was the indispensable fuel for the Commonwealth itself.** It was a matter of national survival and strength, a critical operational input for the entire nation. So, they made a **deliberate policy decision: to find smart ways to encourage risky domestic drilling through the tax code**, laying the groundwork for a century of operational calculus. This abundance of American oil would later be the primary engine that won World War II and powered the entire post-Depression boom—that golden era of American prosperity we all long to recreate.

**II. Operational Design: Leveraging the Tax Code for Rapid Expansion**

The truth about oil isn't just about discovery; it's about the gamble. Every wildcatter, every original **Builder** who sunk a drill bit into the ground, was betting everything against immense odds. Finding oil was high-risk, high-reward. To get more oil, faster, the nation needed to de-risk this massive private investment.

The solution came through a series of laws that created two powerful tools specifically for the oil industry:

* **The first was a massive, permanent tax discount.** It allowed oil producers to write off over a quarter of their gross income from a well, year after year, even after they had recouped their entire original investment. It was a powerful incentive that made successful wells vastly more profitable.  
* **The second allowed an immediate write-off for drilling costs.** It let companies deduct the "intangible" costs of drilling—things like labor, fuel, and site preparation—in the year they were spent, rather than spreading those costs out over time. This dramatically cut the upfront financial hit of drilling a new well, making it easier to risk the capital.

These weren't small tweaks; they were fundamental design choices for the economic machine. They effectively supercharged the industry, stimulating a massive rush of private investment and a rapid expansion of domestic production. The nation’s capacity to "make more stuff" soared. This created a vital national security asset and a foundational input for industrial growth, a direct multiplier for every business and job that relied on cheap, abundant energy. But there was a strategic trade-off. These tax rules were specifically designed to reduce the amount of money oil companies paid back to the nation in taxes, meaning less direct public capital was available for broader **Commonwealth** investments. This approach, while maximizing immediate **Private Wealth**, strategically prioritized rapid resource development above all else.

**III. The Ultimate Lesson: Building the Bedrock of American Prosperity**

The choices made in these early years were clear, and their **benefits for both personal wealth and the Commonwealth were colossal, creating the bedrock of modern American prosperity.** This period stands as a testament to the immense power unleashed by strategically fueling a vital industry.

The immense **benefit** was undeniable: the direct flow of energy that drove America's unprecedented industrial expansion. Imagine the roar of factories in Detroit, the endless miles of railroads connecting a continent, the surge of new inventions, and the millions of jobs created across countless sectors. This energy abundance was the primary engine that allowed America to transform into the global economic powerhouse of the 20th century. It provided the **Commonwealth** with the strategic fuel to win World War I with decisive force, and built the very **economic bedrock** that would serve as the unshakeable foundation for the prosperity of the entire post-Depression era. For the **Builders** of this time, the opportunity to create huge **personal wealth** was unparalleled, a direct outcome of harnessing this raw power.

What you see here is the power of a clear operational logic, even at the dawn of modern economic policy. The leadership of the era, understanding the severe financial limits of the Gold Standard, made confident, strategic decisions to leverage the nascent tax system. They chose to unlock vast private capital, rapidly develop a critical national asset, and fuel the greatest economic expansion in history. This aggressive growth, while creating immense power, would eventually create systemic complexities that demanded greater understanding and recalibration in later eras.

### **Case Study 1: The Great Depression & The New Deal (1929–1939) \- A Machine Without a Manual**

**I. The View from the Penthouse**

To understand the power of an accurate economic control panel, we must first place ourselves inside the machine during its most catastrophic failure. The story begins in the Roaring Twenties, in the gleaming penthouse of a national **Skyscraper** that seemed to have no ceiling. The **Private Wealth Pipe** was a firehose, flooding the **Financial Economy** with easy credit. Fortunes were built not on making things, but on the giddy, upward spiral of the stock market. This was a classic, politically invisible asset price inflation bubble. No one cared, because on paper, everyone with a stake in the game was getting richer by the day.

This silent fire, unchecked by any "brakes" on the control panel, directly funneled resources into speculative assets rather than productive capacity, ultimately weakening the entire foundation before the inevitable collapse. Then, in 1929, the floor fell out. The bubble burst, and the financial structure shattered. The crisis, however, did not remain in the penthouse. It cascaded down with terrifying speed, spreading to the **Real Economy** and bringing the entire national structure to the brink of collapse.

**II. An Unreadable Control Panel**

What followed was a decade of human tragedy. Unemployment soared to a staggering 25%. Factories sat silent; farms withered. For the builders of the day, it was a waking nightmare. The nation’s **real resources**—the skilled workers, the machinery, the fertile land—were not destroyed; they were simply sitting idle, a vast surplus of productive capacity waiting for a command that never came.

The reason for this paralysis can be traced to the fatally flawed and tragically misunderstood control panel of the era. The leaders of the 1930s were not acting out of malice. They were trapped, operating a 20th-century engine with a 19th-century instruction manual. They were shackled to the golden handcuffs of the **Gold Standard**, which forced the government to act as if it could go bankrupt.

As the economy collapsed, their flawed instruments forced them into the exact opposite of what was needed: they cut spending and raised taxes in a desperate panic to protect a number on a spreadsheet while the **Real Economy** bled out. On our control panel, this amounted to applying extreme "brakes" to an already stalled engine, guaranteeing that the nation’s ability to produce anything of value would remain crippled.

**III. The New Deal: Groping for the Controls**

Into this crisis stepped the Roosevelt administration. The New Deal was not a coherent ideology; it was a desperate, pragmatic search for any lever on the control panel that might work. Viewed through our modern instruments, we can see they pulled a contradictory mix of brilliant, flawed, and transformative controls.

Some of their most effective choices were powerful **High-Efficiency Accelerators**. The **Works Progress Administration (WPA)**, for example, put millions of unemployed Americans to work building public assets like schools, bridges, and parks. It was a policy that created new national capacity while instantly transforming millions of tax-draining citizens into tax-paying ones.

Alongside these accelerators, the administration installed crucial new safety systems. The **Glass-Steagall Act** and the creation of the **SEC** were powerful **Systemic Brakes and Guardrails**, designed to prevent the very kind of speculative asset inflation that had destroyed the **Skyscraper**. The **Social Security Act** installed a permanent **Broad Demand Stabilizer** to protect families from future downturns.

However, other experiments were deeply flawed. The **Agricultural Adjustment Act (AAA)** attempted to help suffering farmers by paying them to destroy crops and livestock—an act that sent the **Supply Gauge** plunging into the red. The entire New Deal portfolio was ultimately haunted by the ghost of the **Gold Standard**. They were groping in the dark, held back by the persistent, flawed fear of the deficit. Even their most effective policies were deployed at a scale far too small to fully restart the engine.

**IV. The Ultimate Lesson: A Tragic Education**

The suffering of the Great Depression was not inevitable. It was a choice, born of a profound misunderstanding of the machine. The final, irrefutable proof of this came with the massive, economy-reviving spending of World War II. This spending, which dwarfed the New Deal, was undertaken with only one question in mind: "Do we have the **real resources** to win this war?"

Because we did, the spending did not cause a runaway **inflation** crisis. It ended the Depression. It demonstrated that government spending, when matched with available **real resources**, is a profoundly powerful and disinflationary accelerator for true prosperity. It proved, once and for all, that the government's financial capacity was never the real issue. The real issue was a broken and unreadable control panel. The Depression was, in this sense, a tragic but necessary education in the true nature of our economic power.

This political reality is what made a balanced dashboard possible, defined by two key settings: an open **Commonwealth Pipe** for massive public investment, and powerful brakes to prevent the **Skyscraper’s** penthouse from detaching. ### **Case Study 3: The Day the Rules Changed (1971) \- Unplugging the Machine**

**I. The Sunday Night Bombshell**

On the evening of Sunday, August 15, 1971, President Richard Nixon interrupted the popular TV western *Bonanza* to address the nation. The speech he delivered was a geopolitical bombshell. He announced that the United States was unilaterally "closing the gold window," tearing up the monetary rules that had governed the global economy since the end of World War II.

To most Americans, it was a confusing speech about international finance. In reality, it was a declaration of economic independence. It was the moment America unplugged itself from the old world and dared everyone to follow. It was the day the instruction manual for the American economic machine was fundamentally and irrevocably rewritten.

**II. The Control Panel's Red Line**

To understand why Nixon acted with such shocking audacity, we must look at the control panel of the late 1960s. The Bretton Woods system was bleeding out. For decades, the U.S. had promised to exchange dollars held by foreign governments for gold at a fixed price. But by 1971, due to the immense costs of the Vietnam War and American investment abroad, there were far more dollars in foreign hands than the U.S. had gold in Fort Knox.

The "Gold Reserve" gauge was plunging toward empty. A slow-motion run on the bank was in progress. Nixon was faced with a stark choice: defend the gold peg by intentionally engineering a deep, painful recession that would crush Main Street, or abandon the peg and prioritize the health of the domestic economy.

**III. The Birth of a Fiat Superpower**

Nixon chose sovereignty. His decision was the single, most powerful, real-world proof of our Principle 1: a **sovereign currency issuer**, like the U.S. government, cannot involuntarily go bankrupt. The moment the window was closed, the artificial constraint that had haunted policymakers for centuries was eliminated. The value of the dollar would no longer be determined by the amount of metal in a vault. It would be determined by the full productive capacity of the United States—the output of its factories, the ingenuity of its workers, and the strength of its builders.

In a single evening, America had stumbled into the full, awesome, and terrifying power of its monetary sovereignty. This fundamental shift immediately opened the door for another strategic maneuver that would cement U.S. global financial power: the **Petrodollar System**. Following Nixon's move, the U.S. strategically secured agreements with major oil-producing nations to price all oil exports exclusively in U.S. dollars. This was like installing a new, indirect pump that channeled global wealth directly into the U.S. financial system, strengthening the dollar that *your* business uses and reinforcing America's global financial leverage.

**IV. The Ultimate Lesson: A Superpower We Refused to Understand**

Herein lies the central tragedy of the next fifty years. The operational reality of the American economic machine was transformed, but the mental models of its operators were not. We had been handed the keys to a modern supercar with seemingly unlimited power, but we continued to operate it as if it were a horse and buggy, still haunted by the ghost of a **Gold Standard** that no longer existed.

This act of liberation should have ushered in an era of intelligent economic management. Instead, it created a profound vacuum of understanding. The nation now possessed a financial superpower, but its leaders largely failed to grasp that the true limits were no longer monetary, but purely physical—our collective ability to produce goods and services. It is this confusion that would lead directly to the catastrophic misdiagnosis of the inflationary crisis that was just around the corner.

### **Case Study 4: The Stagflation Era & Regulatory Shift (1970s) \- Misreading the "Check Engine Light"**

**I. The View from the Gas Line**

The party had to end. By the early 1970s, the unshakeable optimism of the post-war era was fading, bogged down in the mire of the Vietnam War. Then, the crisis hit, not on a battlefield, but in daily life. Gas lines snaked for blocks, ending at stations with dreaded "SORRY, NO GAS" signs. The price of everything, from bread to electricity, suddenly shot upward. For the first time in a generation, Americans faced a terrifying new reality: their paychecks were buying less, and their neighbors were losing their jobs, all at the same time. The well-calibrated economic **Pyramid**, which had run so smoothly for so long, was now sputtering, shaking, and threatening to stall completely.

**II. A Broken Control Panel**

In the halls of power, there was intellectual panic. The control panel that had guided the nation through three decades of prosperity was now useless. Just a few years prior, the fundamental wiring of that machine had been irrevocably altered when the nation severed its ties to the gold standard, removing the historical 'golden handcuffs'. Now, the traditional instruments of economic management, especially the Keynesian "Phillips Curve," proved tragically inadequate in this newly unbound system. For a generation, policymakers had navigated by this single, primary gauge on the Keynesian dashboard, which showed them a simple, stable trade-off between **inflation** and unemployment. You could have one or the other, but never both. The 1970s shattered this gauge. With both inflation and unemployment rising at the same time, the old instruction manual was worthless. Policymakers were flying blind, grappling with a monster that their own theories insisted could not exist.

**III. The Policy Portfolio of Panic**

The stagflation crisis stands as the perfect, irrefutable proof of our third principle: **inflation** is the "check engine light" for **real resource** constraints. The crisis was not caused by a sudden surge of "too much money." It was ignited by a massive, external supply shock. **The 1973 OPEC Oil Embargo was the catastrophic "clog in the fuel line" for the entire American economic machine, quadrupling the price of oil almost overnight.** This sent a powerful wave of **cost-push inflation** throughout the economy. The policymakers of the era, staring at their broken control panel, made the fatal error of misdiagnosing the warning light.

Faced with a crisis they couldn't diagnose, leaders reached for a series of flawed and ultimately destructive controls. The first response, under President Nixon, was a desperate attempt to command the problem away. Using authority granted by the **Economic Stabilization Act of 1970**, he imposed direct wage and price controls—a politically expedient, if economically misguided, effort to address the visible symptoms of inflation without understanding its deeper cause. On our control panel, this is the equivalent of putting a piece of black tape over a flashing "check engine" light. The ultimate response, culminating in the "Volcker Shock," was to use the **Main Power Dial** to crush **inflation** by inducing a severe recession. This was a catastrophic misdiagnosis. It was an attempt to fix a clogged fuel line by taking a sledgehammer to the entire engine.

**IV. The Ultimate Lesson: A Faulty Diagnosis Leads to Radical Surgery**

The stagflation crisis was not a failure of the post-war **Pyramid** design. It was a profound failure of diagnosis. The "check engine light" was flashing because of a **real-world supply problem**. The policymakers, using their broken instruments, mistook it for a simple demand problem and deployed a sledgehammer that shattered the economic machine. Because they used the wrong tools with such devastating results, the entire political and intellectual consensus shifted. The conclusion drawn was not "we misread the gauge and used the wrong tool," but "the entire post-war machine is fundamentally broken." This powerful and tragically false narrative of government failure created the perfect opening for a new, radical economic ideology. The first major crack in the foundation appeared with the **Airline Deregulation Act of 1978**. Passed with bipartisan support, it was the first explicit dismantling of a New Deal-era regulatory structure, built on the seductive promise that unleashing market competition was the only way to cure the economy's ills. This act was the crucial harbinger, proving that the intellectual pivot was already underway. The sputtering, misfiring engine of the 1970s was about to be completely rewired according to the **Skyscraper** design, an act that would define the next forty years of American economic life.

You can't solve a supply problem by crushing the people who make the supplies.* |

### **Case Study 5: The Neoliberal Turn (1970s–2000) \- The Skyscraper Design**

**I. The View from the Boardroom**

After the turmoil and self-doubt of the 1970s, a new story was told. It was "Morning in America." The message was one of optimism, strength, and the power of the individual. But beneath the surface of this new dawn, a quiet, radical revolution in economic management was underway. The goal was no longer to repair the sputtering post-war engine. The goal was to rip it out and replace it with a new one, designed according to a completely different plan: the **Skyscraper**.

**II. The Seductive Promise of the Skyscraper**

To understand why this new plan was so widely embraced, we must understand its powerful, seductive promise. The post-war **Pyramid**, for all its success, had come to be seen as slow and bureaucratic. The **Skyscraper** design, in contrast, offered a vision of dynamism. It was a story of unleashing the "animal spirits" of capitalism, of rewarding the risk-takers and the "job creators," and of trusting that their success would, through a kind of financial gravity, "trickle down" and enrich the entire nation.

It was a simple, powerful, and deeply American story. And it was built on a new, brutally simple control panel. The messy, real-world gauges of "Full Employment" and "Median Wage Growth" were thrown out. From this moment on, the nation's prosperity would be measured almost exclusively by the health of the **Financial Economy's** penthouse.

**III. The Policy Portfolio of the Rewiring**

This rewiring was executed with a coordinated set of economic controls, all designed to reverse the flow of the pipes. The signature achievement, the **Economic Recovery Tax Act of 1981**, was a **High-Risk Accelerator** that slashed taxes on top earners and corporations, blasting open the **Private Wealth firehose**. This was followed by a relentless wave of financial deregulation, most impactfully the **Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act of 1999**, which formally repealed the Glass-Steagall Act's guardrails, and the **Commodity Futures Modernization Act of 2000**, which removed oversight from the risky derivatives that would later explode the system.

Crucially, this era also saw a fundamental change in how essential commodities were treated. This is where the traders in the penthouse figured out how to hijack the paperwork.

When you're running a job site, you need a pile of lumber. It's real, you can touch it, you can build with it. The price is supposed to be set by the sawmill and the trucking company. Your **purchase order** is just the paper that says the lumber is coming.

The **financialization of commodities** was the moment Wall Street started trading the **purchase order**, flipping it from one speculator to another, a hundred times over, before the lumber was even loaded on the truck. Each time it flipped, they added a few cents to the price, skimming a profit for doing nothing more than clicking a mouse.

For a real-world example, look at the growth of the **NYMEX crude oil futures market**, which exploded in the 1980s. This transformed physical barrels of oil into sophisticated financial instruments. By the time that oil was ready to be delivered to a refinery to be made into the diesel for your trucks, its price had already been influenced by thousands of digital trades completely disconnected from the physical reality of supply and demand.

The traders used the representation of your materials to create a private toll road, and you were the one who had to pay it. This was paired with an all-out assault on organized labor, a structural change designed to ensure that the wealth generated by the **Real Economy** flowed exclusively upwards.

**IV. The Ultimate Lesson: The Great Detachment**

The rewiring was a stunning success on its own terms. It delivered on its promise to the penthouse. But it broke its promise to the foundation. It led directly to the statistical reality economists call "The Great Divergence"—the forty-year period where the productivity of the American worker continued to climb, while their **real wages** remained flat.

For our purposes, this was the moment of **The Great Detachment**. The policies of the Neoliberal Turn successfully and intentionally unmoored the penthouse of the **Financial Economy** from the foundation of the **Real Economy**. This was not an accident; it was the deliberate outcome of a policy framework that prioritized financial metrics over shared prosperity. The new machine, designed to run without brakes and to funnel all its power to a tiny fraction of its operators, was now on a multi-decade collision course with reality. The 2008 crash was not a fluke. It was an inevitability built into the new machine's design.

### **Case Study 6: The 21st Century & The 2008 Financial Crisis \- The Skyscraper Collapses**

**I. The View from the Foreclosure Sign**

The early 2000s were a quiet, suburban version of the Roaring Twenties' speculative frenzy. The American dream seemed more accessible than ever, built on the seemingly unshakable foundation that house prices only go up. On Wall Street, complex and opaque financial products were the tools used to build a gleaming, ever-taller penthouse for the **Financial Economy**.

Then, the illusion shattered. In September 2008, the investment bank Lehman Brothers collapsed, and the house of cards came tumbling down. The crisis wasn't just on a computer screen in Manhattan; it was on Main Street, as "For Sale" signs morphed into foreclosure notices on lawn after lawn. The **Skyscraper**, which had been redlining for years on the fuel of risky debt, was now collapsing onto the foundation below. This was the predictable structural failure of the design that had been meticulously constructed over the previous decades.

**II. A Control Panel Blind to the Blaze**

For years, policymakers had celebrated a period of low consumer price **inflation**, viewing the massive asset inflation bubble in housing not as a danger, but as the primary indicator of success. Their control panel was fatally flawed. It was a fire alarm designed to only detect smoke, not heat. Obsessed with the small, contained flame of consumer prices, it was completely blind to the raging, invisible inferno of the asset bubble that was consuming the entire structure. The machine was shaking violently, but the official dashboard insisted everything was fine.

**III. The Policy Portfolio of Triage**

In the panic of September 2008, the decades-long narrative of the **"deficit myth"** evaporated overnight. The U.S. government demonstrated its unlimited capacity to create the dollars needed to save the system. The question was never "can we afford to stop the collapse?" The question was "who should we save?"

The immense power of the public purse was unleashed, but the control panel guiding it was still the one that prioritized the **Skyscraper's** penthouse. The financial bailout, primarily through the **Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP)** and the Fed's **Quantitative Easing (QE)**, was a **High-Risk Policy: Penthouse Rescue**. It was a multi-trillion-dollar firehose aimed exclusively at the largest financial institutions. In contrast, the **American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009 (ARRA)**, the primary policy designed to help Main Street, was a garden hose sent to fight a five-alarm fire. Hamstrung by the persistent fear of the deficit, it was far too small to fill the enormous economic hole created by the crash.

**IV. The Ultimate Lesson: A Political Choice Revealed**

The 2008 crisis and its aftermath were the ultimate validation of the **"Two Economies"** thesis. The response proved that the government's capacity to act was never the real issue. It revealed that the use of that immense power is a fundamentally political choice. The government's triage—prioritizing the asset holders over the wage earners, saving the penthouse over the foundation—was a profound political choice. This decision resulted in a slow, weak, and deeply unequal recovery that massively reinforced the **Great Detachment**, setting the stage for a decade of populist anger and political instability. The **Skyscraper** was saved, but it was now clearer than ever who the machine was really built to serve.

### **Case Study 7: The Modern Era & The Pandemic Response \- The Final Stress Test**

**I. The View from the Empty Street**

In March 2020, the world fell silent. This was not a financial crash that started on a computer screen. This was a physical shutdown. Restaurants, airlines, factories, and schools all hit the emergency brake. The global economic engine didn't sputter; it seized up entirely. The crisis was not economic in origin; it was a fundamental constraint on our most precious **real resource**: human health.

In a stunning reversal from 2008, the sheer universality of the crisis galvanized a rare political consensus. The policy response was immediate, massive, and, crucially, directed straight at the **Real Economy**. The hard lesson from the last crisis had finally resonated: in a moment of existential threat, you cannot save the foundation by only saving the penthouse. The **Commonwealth Pipe** was opened at full blast.

**II. The Great MMT Experiment**

The COVID-19 pandemic served as the ultimate, real-world stress test for our framework, proving all three of our core principles at once.

Principle 1 was proven on a scale previously unimaginable. The multi-trillion-dollar **CARES Act**, an act of **Emergency Broad Support**, was passed with zero discussion of "paying for it." The **"deficit myth,"** which had dominated policy for forty years, simply vanished.

Principles 2 and 3 were then proven in the chaotic aftermath. The subsequent **inflation** was the perfect, real-world laboratory experiment. It was a clear collision between a massive amount of new spending (**financial resources**) and a global economy whose supply chains were simultaneously breaking down (a severe constraint on **real resources**). The "check engine light" of inflation flashed red, not because of "too much money," but because of a fundamental mismatch between our demand for goods and our temporarily crippled ability to produce and deliver them.

This period also delivered the most humiliating evidence of the **Skyscraper's** brittleness: in April 2020, the price of U.S. crude oil went negative. Producers were literally paying buyers to take oil off their hands because global demand had evaporated and physical storage was full. It was a catastrophic failure of a market that had become dangerously detached from physical reality.

**III. The Ultimate Lesson: The Control Panel is Real**

The COVID response proved two things with stunning clarity. First, the MMT description of sovereign government capacity is correct: we can afford to protect our population in a crisis, and the austerity that followed 2008 was a political choice, not a necessity.

Second, the control panel is not a video game. The real-world limits of productive capacity are very real. The collision of necessary government spending with a broken global supply chain demonstrated that the true constraint on our prosperity is not a line in a budget, but the availability of **real resources**, materials, and labor.

The debate for the next decade, reflected in legislation like the **Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (2021)** and the **CHIPS and Science Act (2022)**, will be which lesson we choose to learn. Do we retreat in fear back to the failed austerity of the past? Or do we finally learn to become skilled operators, using the full power of our control panel to make targeted, high-efficiency, disinflationary investments in our own **productive capacity**—building an economy that is not only prosperous, but resilient?
